\tzpsection{Derivation Layer}
\paragraph{Primary Equation.}
\begin{equation}
\exists D
\quad\text{such that}\quad
P(D\mid H)\neq P(D\mid \neg H).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Primary Derivation.}
A hypothesis $H$ is falsifiable only if there exists some possible observation $D$ that would distinguish $H$ from its negation.

If every possible observation had equal probability under both $H$ and $\neg H$, then no data could discriminate between them:

\begin{equation}
P(D\mid H)=P(D\mid \neg H)
\quad
\text{for all }D .
\end{equation}

Therefore, falsifiability requires at least one possible discriminating datum:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\exists D
\quad\text{such that}\quad
P(D\mid H)\neq P(D\mid \neg H).
}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
A Trawinistic hypothesis must produce at least one observational consequence that differs from its negation.
