\paragraph{Canonical Statement.}
\#\# 2. Can a surface “lap itself”? - You’re imagining a point on the star moving so fast that it overtakes where it *was* a moment ago. - **In classical physics**, sure, if the angular velocity is high enough, a point could theoretically rotate more than 360° in the time you define as one rotation. - **But relativity intervenes:** - The surface cannot exceed the **speed of light (c)**. - For a star of radius \textbackslash{}( R \textbackslash{}), the tangential speed at the equator is: \textbackslash{}[ v = \textbackslash{}omega R \textbackslash{}] - If \textbackslash{}( \textbackslash{}omega \textbackslash{}) is too high, \textbackslash{}( v > c \textbackslash{}) would occur — **impossible** according to special relativity.

\paragraph{Canonical Equation.}
\[
v = \omega R
